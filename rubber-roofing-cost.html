So you're considering installing a new rubber roof on your home or commercial building. Smart move—rubber roofing is durable, energy-efficient, and eco-friendly. But how much is this upgrade going to set you back? The costs can vary quite a bit depending on the type of rubber roofing you choose and the size of your roof.

Rubber Roof Replacement Costs: EPDM, TPO, or PVC

The cost of a rubber roof installation depends on several factors, but you can expect to pay between $10 to $20 per square foot.

EPDM rubber roofing, also known as black rubber roofing, is the most affordable option. It's a durable, waterproof material made of ethylene propylene diene terpolymer synthetic rubber. You'll pay $10-$15 per square foot for EPDM rubber roofing installation.

TPO roofing, or thermoplastic polyolefin, is a popular energy-efficient choice. It's white, so it reflects sunlight and helps insulate your home. TPO rubber roofing installation runs $12-$18 per square foot.

PVC, or polyvinyl chloride, roofing is another long-lasting, low-maintenance option. PVC roofing comes in different colors and styles, but installation costs are the highest at $15-$20 per square foot.

While rubber roofing installation is more expensive initially, it can save you money in the long run, thanks to its durability and energy efficiency. The waterproof, fire-resistant material requires little maintenance and can withstand extreme weather without needing replacement for decades. If you want an eco-friendly roof that will protect your home for years to come, rubber roofing is worth the investment.

Factors That Affect the Cost of a Rubber Roof

The cost of your rubber roof can vary greatly depending on several factors.

  • The type of rubber roofing material you choose, like EPDM, TPO, or PVC, will impact the price.
  • The size and complexity of your roof will affect the cost. A larger and more intricate roof will be more expensive compared to a smaller and simpler flat roof.
  • Where you live plays a role too. Rubber roof installation costs tend to be higher in areas with a high cost of living. Labor and materials cost more in those places.
  • Additional features like insulation, roof vents, or skylights will increase the cost—the more add-ons and extras, the higher the price tag.
  • The experience and reputation of your roofing contractor influence the cost. An established roofing contractor with good reviews and a proven track record of quality work will charge more than a new company still building their reputation.

Rubber roofing installation may not be inexpensive, but its extended lifespan and low maintenance can save long-term costs. Although the initial investment may seem high, a well-installed rubber roof can last 50 years or more without requiring replacement, making it an economical option.

Save Money on Your Rubber Roof Installation

Don't go with the first roofing contractor. Get multiple bids from different companies to compare costs. Prices can vary significantly between roofers for the same job. Ask each contractor precisely what is included in their quote to ensure you compare apples to apples. Look for hidden fees or extras that may increase the overall cost.

Consider DIY for Small Repairs

If there are any small leaks or minor damage to your existing rubber roof, you can fix it to extend its life a few more years. Patch kits for EPDM and TPO roofs can seal small holes and cracks. As long as the roof membrane is still in good shape, these repairs are easy enough for a DIYer to accomplish. Of course, professional replacement is best for major damage or if your roof is near the end of its lifespan.

Time Your Installation Strategically

Like any construction project, roofing contractors are busier during peak season in the summer. If you can wait until the off-season in the fall or winter, you can get a lower price because contractors are less busy. They'll be happy with the work and more willing to offer a competitive rate. You'll also avoid longer wait times to schedule the installation.
